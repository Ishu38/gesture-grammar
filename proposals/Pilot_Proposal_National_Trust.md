## 1. Problem Statement

An estimated 2.4 million children in India live with cerebral palsy (ICMR, 2020), and a significant proportion have limited access to structured English literacy education. Traditional teaching methods rely on writing, typing, or verbal repetition — modalities that are partially or fully inaccessible to children with motor impairments, non-verbal autism, or hearing disabilities.

Special educators in government and trust-run schools report a critical gap: **there is no affordable, self-paced, motor-adaptive tool for teaching English sentence structure to children with physical disabilities.**

## 2. Proposed Solution

**MLAF (Multimodal Language Acquisition Framework)** is a browser-based application that teaches English grammar through hand gestures detected by a standard phone or laptop camera.

**How it works:**
- The child performs hand gestures in front of a camera
- A trained neural network (CNN, 99.5% accuracy) recognizes 19 gestures mapped to English words (subjects, verbs, objects)
- The child builds sentences gesture-by-gesture: e.g., "He grabs apple"
- A formal grammar engine validates sentence structure in real time (Subject-Verb-Object order, verb agreement)
- The system speaks the completed sentence aloud (Text-to-Speech) — becoming the child's voice

**Key adaptive features for this population:**
- **Motor calibration:** Detects hand tremor and adjusts recognition tolerance per learner
- **Cognitive load monitoring:** Lowers difficulty when the child shows signs of fatigue
- **ISL interference correction:** Detects when deaf learners default to Indian Sign Language word order (SOV) and guides them toward English SVO structure
- **Spaced repetition:** Revisits gestures the child struggles with, following evidence-based memory schedules

**Requirements:** A laptop or tablet with a camera. No internet required after initial load. No specialized hardware.

## 3. Pilot Study Design

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Participants** | 30 children (ages 6-14) with cerebral palsy, non-verbal autism, or hearing impairment |
| **Sites** | 2 special schools under National Trust network |
| **Duration** | 8 weeks (3 sessions per week, 20 minutes per session) |
| **Design** | Pre-test / post-test with control group (15 intervention, 15 control) |
| **Primary outcome** | Improvement in English sentence construction ability (SVO ordering accuracy) |
| **Secondary outcomes** | Gesture recognition speed (automaticity), learner engagement (session duration), motor adaptation effectiveness |
| **Assessment tool** | Custom 20-item sentence construction test administered via the app, scored automatically |
| **Data collection** | All session data logged locally on device (no cloud, no personal data transmitted) |
| **Ethics** | Parental consent required; study protocol to be reviewed by institutional ethics committee |

## 5. Expected Outcomes

1. Quantitative evidence on whether gesture-based grammar instruction improves English sentence construction in motor-impaired children
2. Usability data: which adaptive features (tremor calibration, cognitive load adaptation) had measurable impact
3. A peer-reviewable dataset for future research on multimodal assistive EdTech
4. Recommendations for integration into National Trust school curricula
